I totally agree.

Due to the curse of dimensionality of high-dimensional spaces, for 200-400dimensions, most points are distributed near the spherical shell. This causes the Euclidean distance difficult to compare the difference between different points in high dimensional spaces.

Cosine similarity measures the angle of the space vector, and it is more reflected in the difference in direction, rather than position. Euclidean distance can reflect the absolute difference of individual numerical characteristics, so it is more used for analysis that needs to reflect the difference from the numerical size of the dimension. Thus cosine similarity is a better than Euclidean similarity in this case.
