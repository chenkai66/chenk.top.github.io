## The big picture

You have a polynomial. You want its roots. For quadratics, there's the quadratic formula. Cubics and quartics have formulas too (ugly, but they exist). Quintics? Abel and Ruffini proved no general formula exists. Galois theory explains *why* — by translating the question into group theory.

The core idea: attach a group to a polynomial (its Galois group). The polynomial is solvable by radicals iff that group is a solvable group. The symmetric group $S_5$ is not solvable, and a generic quintic has Galois group $S_5$. Done.

## The Galois group

Let $K/F$ be a field extension. An **$F$-automorphism** of $K$ is a field isomorphism $\sigma: K \to K$ that fixes $F$ pointwise: $\sigma(a) = a$ for all $a \in F$.

The **Galois group** $\text{Gal}(K/F)$ is the set of all $F$-automorphisms of $K$, forming a group under composition.

$$
\text{Gal}(K/F) = \{\sigma: K \xrightarrow{\sim} K \mid \sigma|_F = \text{id}_F\}
$$

An extension is **Galois** if it is both normal (splitting field of some polynomial) and separable. For Galois extensions:

$$
|\text{Gal}(K/F)| = [K:F]
$$

### Example 1: $\text{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})/\mathbb{Q})$

Any $\mathbb{Q}$-automorphism $\sigma$ of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$ is determined by $\sigma(\sqrt{2})$. Since $\sigma$ preserves the relation $(\sqrt{2})^2 = 2$, we need $\sigma(\sqrt{2})^2 = 2$, so $\sigma(\sqrt{2}) = \pm\sqrt{2}$.

The two automorphisms: the identity and conjugation $a + b\sqrt{2} \mapsto a - b\sqrt{2}$. The Galois group is $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

### Example 2: $\text{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)/\mathbb{Q})$

This is the splitting field of $x^3 - 2$ with degree 6 over $\mathbb{Q}$. The Galois group has order 6. An automorphism is determined by where it sends $\sqrt[3]{2}$ (three choices: $\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega^2\sqrt[3]{2}$) and $\omega$ (two choices: $\omega, \omega^2$). This gives $3 \times 2 = 6$ automorphisms.

The Galois group is isomorphic to $S_3$: it acts faithfully on the three roots of $x^3 - 2$ by permuting them.

## The Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory

**Theorem.** Let $K/F$ be a finite Galois extension with Galois group $G = \text{Gal}(K/F)$. There is an inclusion-reversing bijection:

$$
\{\text{intermediate fields } F \subseteq L \subseteq K\} \longleftrightarrow \{\text{subgroups } H \leq G\}
$$

given by:
- $L \mapsto H = \text{Gal}(K/L)$ (automorphisms fixing $L$)
- $H \mapsto L = K^H$ (elements of $K$ fixed by all of $H$)

Moreover:
- $[K:L] = |H|$ and $[L:F] = [G:H]$
- $L/F$ is Galois iff $H \trianglelefteq G$, and then $\text{Gal}(L/F) \cong G/H$

This is remarkable. Questions about field extensions (infinite, messy, hard to compute) become questions about finite groups (combinatorial, tractable).

### Example 3: Intermediate fields of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3})/\mathbb{Q}$

The degree is 4. The Galois group is $V_4 = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ (the Klein four-group), generated by $\sigma: \sqrt{2} \mapsto -\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3} \mapsto \sqrt{3}$ and $\tau: \sqrt{2} \mapsto \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3} \mapsto -\sqrt{3}$.

$V_4$ has three subgroups of order 2: $\langle\sigma\rangle$, $\langle\tau\rangle$, $\langle\sigma\tau\rangle$. The corresponding fixed fields are:
- $K^{\langle\sigma\rangle} = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{3})$ (fixed by negating $\sqrt{2}$)
- $K^{\langle\tau\rangle} = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$ (fixed by negating $\sqrt{3}$)
- $K^{\langle\sigma\tau\rangle} = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{6})$ (fixed by negating both)

Three intermediate quadratic fields, matching three index-2 subgroups.

## Solvability by radicals

A polynomial $f \in F[x]$ is **solvable by radicals** if its roots can be expressed using field operations ($+, -, \times, \div$) and $n$-th roots, starting from $F$.

A group $G$ is **solvable** if it has a composition series with abelian quotients:

$$
\{e\} = G_0 \trianglelefteq G_1 \trianglelefteq \cdots \trianglelefteq G_n = G, \quad G_{i+1}/G_i \text{ abelian}
$$

**Theorem (Galois).** A polynomial $f \in F[x]$ (char $F = 0$) is solvable by radicals iff $\text{Gal}(\text{Split}_F(f)/F)$ is a solvable group.

**Why quintics fail.** The group $S_5$ is not solvable. Its only nontrivial normal subgroup is $A_5$, which is simple (no normal subgroups). Since $A_5$ is non-abelian simple, no composition series with abelian quotients exists. A generic degree-5 polynomial has Galois group $S_5$, so it cannot be solved by radicals.

**Concretely:** $f(x) = x^5 - 4x + 2$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ (Eisenstein at $p=2$). It has exactly three real roots (check: $f'(x) = 5x^4 - 4$ has two real critical points). A degree-5 irreducible with exactly three real roots has Galois group $S_5$. So this specific polynomial has no radical formula for its roots.

## Computing Galois groups

In practice, determining $\text{Gal}(f)$ for a specific polynomial involves:
1. Finding the degree of the splitting field
2. Checking transitivity of the action on roots
3. Using the discriminant: $\text{Gal}(f) \subseteq A_n$ iff the discriminant is a perfect square in $F$

For cubics over $\mathbb{Q}$: the Galois group is either $S_3$ (discriminant not a perfect square) or $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ (discriminant is a perfect square).

The discriminant of $x^3 + px + q$ is $\Delta = -4p^3 - 27q^2$. For $x^3 - 2$: $\Delta = -108$. Not a perfect square, so $\text{Gal}(x^3 - 2/\mathbb{Q}) = S_3$.
