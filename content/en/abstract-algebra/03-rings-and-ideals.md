## From one operation to two

Groups capture a single operation with invertibility. But most mathematical objects we work with daily — integers, polynomials, matrices — come with two operations: addition and multiplication. A **ring** is the framework that handles both.

## The definition

A **ring** $(R, +, \cdot)$ is a set with two operations such that:

$$
(R, +) \text{ is an abelian group (identity } 0, \text{ inverse } {-a}\text{)}
$$

$$
\text{(Associativity of } \cdot\text{)} \quad (ab)c = a(bc)
$$

$$
\text{(Distributivity)} \quad a(b+c) = ab + ac, \quad (a+b)c = ac + bc
$$

If there exists $1 \in R$ with $1 \cdot a = a \cdot 1 = a$ for all $a$, we say $R$ is a **ring with unity**. If $ab = ba$ for all $a, b$, the ring is **commutative**.

### Example 1: $\mathbb{Z}$ and $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$

The integers form a commutative ring with unity. So does $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ with addition and multiplication mod $n$. Something interesting happens: in $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$, we have $2 \cdot 3 = 0$ even though $2 \neq 0$ and $3 \neq 0$. Elements like these are called **zero divisors**.

A commutative ring with unity and no zero divisors is an **integral domain**. The integers $\mathbb{Z}$ are an integral domain. $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$ is not.

**Key observation.** $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ is an integral domain (in fact, a field) iff $p$ is prime. If $p$ is prime and $ab \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$, then $p \mid ab$, so $p \mid a$ or $p \mid b$.

## Ideals: the ring-theoretic normal subgroups

In group theory, normal subgroups let you form quotients. In ring theory, the analogous objects are **ideals**.

A subset $I \subseteq R$ is an **ideal** (two-sided) if:

$$
I \text{ is an additive subgroup of } R, \quad \text{and} \quad rI \subseteq I,\; Ir \subseteq I \;\;\forall\, r \in R
$$

That last condition — closure under multiplication by arbitrary ring elements — is stronger than being a subring. It's what makes the quotient $R/I$ inherit a ring structure.

### Example 2: Principal ideals in $\mathbb{Z}$

For any $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, the set $n\mathbb{Z} = \{\ldots, -2n, -n, 0, n, 2n, \ldots\}$ is an ideal. The quotient ring $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is exactly the ring of integers mod $n$.

In fact, every ideal of $\mathbb{Z}$ has this form — $\mathbb{Z}$ is a **principal ideal domain** (PID). The ideal generated by $n$ is written $(n)$.

## Quotient rings

Given an ideal $I \trianglelefteq R$, the **quotient ring** $R/I$ consists of cosets $r + I$ with operations:

$$
(r_1 + I) + (r_2 + I) = (r_1 + r_2) + I, \quad (r_1 + I)(r_2 + I) = (r_1 r_2) + I
$$

**Ring Isomorphism Theorem.** If $\varphi: R \to S$ is a ring homomorphism, then $R/\ker\varphi \cong \text{im}\,\varphi$.

### Example 3: $\mathbb{R}[x]/(x^2 + 1) \cong \mathbb{C}$

Take the polynomial ring $\mathbb{R}[x]$ and quotient by the ideal $(x^2 + 1)$. In the quotient, $x^2 + 1 = 0$, so $x^2 = -1$. The coset $x + (x^2+1)$ behaves exactly like $i$. Every element of the quotient has the form $a + bx$ (reduce any polynomial mod $x^2+1$), and multiplication follows the rule $(a+bx)(c+dx) = (ac-bd) + (ad+bc)x$. This is precisely $\mathbb{C}$.

## Polynomial rings

The ring $R[x]$ consists of polynomials with coefficients in $R$. If $R$ is commutative with unity, so is $R[x]$. The degree function satisfies $\deg(fg) = \deg f + \deg g$ when $R$ is an integral domain.

**Division algorithm.** If $F$ is a field and $f, g \in F[x]$ with $g \neq 0$, then there exist unique $q, r \in F[x]$ with:

$$
f = qg + r, \quad \deg r < \deg g
$$

This makes $F[x]$ a Euclidean domain. You can run the Euclidean algorithm to find GCDs of polynomials, exactly as with integers.

**Theorem.** $F[x]$ is a PID: every ideal is generated by a single polynomial. Moreover, $F[x]$ is a unique factorization domain (UFD): every non-constant polynomial factors uniquely into irreducibles (up to units and ordering).

*Proof sketch.* Let $I$ be a nonzero ideal in $F[x]$. Pick $g \in I$ of minimal degree. For any $f \in I$, divide: $f = qg + r$ with $\deg r < \deg g$. Then $r = f - qg \in I$, contradicting minimality unless $r = 0$. So $I = (g)$. $\square$

### Example 4: Irreducibility over different fields

The polynomial $x^2 + 1$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{R}$ (no real roots), but factors as $(x+i)(x-i)$ over $\mathbb{C}$. Over $\mathbb{F}_2 = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, we have $x^2 + 1 = (x+1)^2$ since $-1 = 1$ in $\mathbb{F}_2$.

Whether a polynomial is irreducible depends entirely on which field the coefficients live in. This observation leads directly to field extensions.

## Prime and maximal ideals

An ideal $P \subset R$ (proper) is **prime** if $ab \in P$ implies $a \in P$ or $b \in P$.

An ideal $M \subset R$ (proper) is **maximal** if no ideal sits strictly between $M$ and $R$.

$$
P \text{ is prime} \iff R/P \text{ is an integral domain}
$$

$$
M \text{ is maximal} \iff R/M \text{ is a field}
$$

Since every field is an integral domain, every maximal ideal is prime. The converse fails: $(0)$ is prime in $\mathbb{Z}$ (since $\mathbb{Z}$ is a domain) but not maximal (since $\mathbb{Z}$ is not a field).

In $\mathbb{Z}$, the maximal ideals are exactly $(p)$ for prime $p$. This matches: $\mathbb{Z}/(p) = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ is a field.
