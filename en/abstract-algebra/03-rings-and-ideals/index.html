Rings, Ideals, and Polynomial Arithmetic

Rings have two operations. Ideals let you build quotient rings. Polynomial rings are the workhorse of algebra.

From one operation to two#

Groups capture a single operation with invertibility. But most mathematical objects we work with daily — integers, polynomials, matrices — come with two operations: addition and multiplication. A ring is the framework that handles both.

The shift from groups to rings is not merely “add another operation.” The interplay between addition and multiplication through distributivity creates phenomena that have no group-theoretic analog: zero divisors, prime factorization, polynomial division. If Part 1 and Part 2 were about the algebra of symmetry, this article is about the algebra of arithmetic.

The definition#

A ring $(R, +, \cdot)$ is a set with two operations such that:

$$ (R, +) \text{ is an abelian group (identity } 0, \text{ inverse } {-a}\text{)} $$ $$ \text{(Associativity of } \cdot\text{)} \quad (ab)c = a(bc) $$ $$ \text{(Distributivity)} \quad a(b+c) = ab + ac, \quad (a+b)c = ac + bc $$

If there exists $1 \in R$ with $1 \cdot a = a \cdot 1 = a$ for all $a$ , we say $R$ is a ring with unity (or unital ring). If $ab = ba$ for all $a, b$ , the ring is commutative. In this series, all rings are commutative with unity unless stated otherwise.

An immediate consequence of the axioms: $0 \cdot a = 0$ for all $a$ . Proof: $0 \cdot a = (0 + 0) \cdot a = 0 \cdot a + 0 \cdot a$ , and canceling (using the additive group structure) gives $0 \cdot a = 0$ . Similarly, $(-1) \cdot a = -a$ .

Example 1: $\mathbb{Z}$ and $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ #

The integers form a commutative ring with unity. So does $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ with addition and multiplication mod $n$ . Something interesting happens: in $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$ , we have $2 \cdot 3 = 0$ even though $2 \neq 0$ and $3 \neq 0$ . Elements like these are called zero divisors.

A commutative ring with unity and no zero divisors is an integral domain. The integers $\mathbb{Z}$ are an integral domain. $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$ is not.

Key observation. $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ is an integral domain (in fact, a field) iff $p$ is prime. If $p$ is prime and $ab \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$ , then $p \mid ab$ , so $p \mid a$ or $p \mid b$ (by Euclid’s lemma).

Non-example. The set $2\mathbb{Z}$ (even integers) under the usual operations is a ring without unity — there is no integer $e$ with $e \cdot n = n$ for all even $n$ , since $1 \notin 2\mathbb{Z}$ . This shows the unity axiom is independent of the others.

Example 2: Matrix rings#

The set $M_n(\mathbb{R})$ of $n \times n$ real matrices is a ring with unity (the identity matrix $I_n$ ). For $n \geq 2$ , it is non-commutative and has zero divisors. For instance in $M_2(\mathbb{R})$ :

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Neither factor is zero, but their product is.

Ideals: the ring-theoretic normal subgroups#

In group theory, normal subgroups let you form quotients. In ring theory, the analogous objects are ideals. The parallel is tight: a normal subgroup $N \trianglelefteq G$ absorbs conjugation ($gNg^{-1} \subseteq N$ ); an ideal $I \trianglelefteq R$ absorbs multiplication ($rI \subseteq I$ ).

A subset $I \subseteq R$ is an ideal if:

$$ I \text{ is an additive subgroup of } R, \quad \text{and} \quad rI \subseteq I \;\;\forall\, r \in R $$

(In a commutative ring, left and right ideals coincide, so we just say “ideal.”)

The condition $rI \subseteq I$ is stronger than being a subring. For instance, $\mathbb{Z}$ is a subring of $\mathbb{Q}$ , but not an ideal — $\frac{1}{2} \cdot 1 = \frac{1}{2} \notin \mathbb{Z}$ .

Example 3: Principal ideals in $\mathbb{Z}$ #

For any $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ , the set $n\mathbb{Z} = \{\ldots, -2n, -n, 0, n, 2n, \ldots\}$ is an ideal. The quotient ring $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is exactly the ring of integers mod $n$ .

In fact, every ideal of $\mathbb{Z}$ has this form — $\mathbb{Z}$ is a principal ideal domain (PID). The ideal generated by $n$ is written $(n)$ . More generally, the ideal generated by elements $a_1, \ldots, a_k$ is $(a_1, \ldots, a_k) = \{r_1 a_1 + \cdots + r_k a_k : r_i \in R\}$ .

Quotient rings and the isomorphism theorem#

Given an ideal $I \trianglelefteq R$ , the quotient ring $R/I$ consists of cosets $r + I$ with operations:

$$ (r_1 + I) + (r_2 + I) = (r_1 + r_2) + I, \quad (r_1 + I)(r_2 + I) = (r_1 r_2) + I $$

The ideal condition guarantees that multiplication is well-defined. If $r_1 + I = r_1' + I$ (meaning $r_1 - r_1' \in I$ ), then $r_1 r_2 - r_1' r_2 = (r_1 - r_1')r_2 \in I$ , so $r_1 r_2 + I = r_1' r_2 + I$ .

Ring Isomorphism Theorem. If $\varphi: R \to S$ is a ring homomorphism (preserving both operations and the unity), then $\ker\varphi$ is an ideal of $R$ , and:

$$ R/\ker\varphi \cong \text{im}\,\varphi $$

Example 4: Constructing $\mathbb{C}$ from $\mathbb{R}[x]$ #

Take the polynomial ring $\mathbb{R}[x]$ and quotient by the ideal $(x^2 + 1)$ . In the quotient, $x^2 + 1 = 0$ , so $x^2 = -1$ . The coset $x + (x^2+1)$ behaves exactly like $i$ . Every element of the quotient has the form $a + bx$ (reduce any polynomial mod $x^2+1$ using the division algorithm), and multiplication follows the rule $(a+bx)(c+dx) = (ac-bd) + (ad+bc)x$ . This is precisely $\mathbb{C}$ .

This construction is paradigmatic: to adjoin a root of a polynomial $p(x)$ to a ring, quotient by $(p(x))$ . We will exploit this systematically in Part 4 (Fields and Extensions) .

Polynomial rings in depth#

The ring $R[x]$ consists of polynomials $a_0 + a_1 x + \cdots + a_n x^n$ with coefficients in $R$ . If $R$ is commutative with unity, so is $R[x]$ . The degree function satisfies $\deg(fg) = \deg f + \deg g$ when $R$ is an integral domain (this can fail when $R$ has zero divisors: in $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}[x]$ , $(2x)(2x) = 4x^2 = 0$ ).

Division algorithm. If $F$ is a field and $f, g \in F[x]$ with $g \neq 0$ , then there exist unique $q, r \in F[x]$ with:

$$ f = qg + r, \quad \deg r < \deg g $$

The proof is the polynomial long-division algorithm you learned in high school — but now it is a theorem, not just a technique. Crucially, we need $F$ to be a field so that we can divide by the leading coefficient of $g$ .

Non-example. In $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ , we cannot always divide. Try dividing $x$ by $2x + 1$ : there is no $q(x) \in \mathbb{Z}[x]$ with $x = q(x)(2x+1) + r$ and $\deg r < 1$ . So $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ does NOT have a division algorithm, and is NOT a PID (the ideal $(2, x)$ requires two generators).

Theorem. $F[x]$ is a PID (hence a UFD): every ideal is generated by a single polynomial, and every non-constant polynomial factors uniquely into irreducibles.

Proof that $F[x]$ is a PID. Let $I$ be a nonzero ideal in $F[x]$ . Pick $g \in I$ of minimal degree. For any $f \in I$ , divide: $f = qg + r$ with $\deg r < \deg g$ . Then $r = f - qg \in I$ . By minimality of $\deg g$ , we must have $r = 0$ . So $f = qg \in (g)$ , giving $I = (g)$ . $\square$

Irreducibility over different fields#

The polynomial $x^2 + 1$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{R}$ (no real roots), but factors as $(x+i)(x-i)$ over $\mathbb{C}$ . Over $\mathbb{F}_2 = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ , we have $x^2 + 1 = (x+1)^2$ since $-1 = 1$ in characteristic 2.

The polynomial $x^4 + 1$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ but factors over every $\mathbb{F}_p$ . Over $\mathbb{F}_2$ : $x^4 + 1 = (x+1)^4$ . Over $\mathbb{F}_3$ : $x^4 + 1 = (x^2 + x - 1)(x^2 - x - 1)$ . This shows that irreducibility is subtle — it depends on the coefficient field in non-obvious ways.

Eisenstein’s Criterion. Let $f(x) = a_n x^n + \cdots + a_0 \in \mathbb{Z}[x]$ . If there is a prime $p$ such that $p \nmid a_n$ , $p \mid a_i$ for $i < n$ , and $p^2 \nmid a_0$ , then $f$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ .

Example. $x^4 + 10x^3 + 15x^2 + 20x + 5$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ by Eisenstein with $p = 5$ .

The Chinese Remainder Theorem for rings#

The Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT), which appeared in group form in Part 1, has a clean ring-theoretic statement.

Theorem (CRT). If $I$ and $J$ are ideals of a commutative ring $R$ with $I + J = R$ (we say $I$ and $J$ are comaximal), then:

$$ R/(I \cap J) \cong R/I \times R/J $$

In $\mathbb{Z}$ : if $\gcd(m, n) = 1$ , then $(m) + (n) = \mathbb{Z}$ (by Bezout’s identity) and $(m) \cap (n) = (mn)$ , giving:

$$ \mathbb{Z}/mn\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} $$

Worked example. Solve the system $x \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$ , $x \equiv 3 \pmod{5}$ . By CRT, this has a unique solution mod 15. We need $x = 3k + 2$ with $3k + 2 \equiv 3 \pmod{5}$ , so $3k \equiv 1 \pmod{5}$ , giving $k \equiv 2 \pmod{5}$ . Hence $x = 3(2) + 2 = 8$ . Check: $8 = 2 + 2 \cdot 3$ and $8 = 3 + 1 \cdot 5$ .

The CRT is the algebraic backbone of modular arithmetic in cryptography (see Part 6 ) and the theory of algebraic number fields.

Prime and maximal ideals#

An ideal $P \subsetneq R$ is prime if $ab \in P$ implies $a \in P$ or $b \in P$ .

An ideal $M \subsetneq R$ is maximal if no ideal sits strictly between $M$ and $R$ .

The connection to quotient structure is clean:

$$ P \text{ is prime} \iff R/P \text{ is an integral domain} $$ $$ M \text{ is maximal} \iff R/M \text{ is a field} $$

Proof sketch (maximal $\Rightarrow$ field). Suppose $M$ is maximal and $r + M \neq 0$ in $R/M$ (i.e., $r \notin M$ ). Then $M + (r) = R$ by maximality (since $M \subsetneq M + (r) \subseteq R$ and $M$ is maximal). So $1 = m + sr$ for some $m \in M$ and $s \in R$ . Then $sr + M = 1 + M$ , meaning $(s+M)(r+M) = 1 + M$ . Every nonzero element is invertible. $\square$

Since every field is an integral domain, every maximal ideal is prime. The converse fails: $(0)$ is prime in $\mathbb{Z}$ (since $\mathbb{Z}$ is a domain) but not maximal (since $\mathbb{Z}$ is not a field — $(0) \subsetneq (2) \subsetneq \mathbb{Z}$ ).

In $\mathbb{Z}$ , the prime ideals are $(0)$ and $(p)$ for prime $p$ ; the maximal ideals are $(p)$ for prime $p$ only.

In $F[x]$ for a field $F$ : the prime (equivalently, maximal) ideals are exactly $(p(x))$ where $p(x)$ is irreducible. This is because $F[x]$ is a PID, and in a PID every nonzero prime ideal is maximal.

Localization (a glimpse)#

Sometimes we want to “invert” certain elements of a ring without going all the way to a field. Localization does this formally.

Given a commutative ring $R$ and a multiplicative set $S \subseteq R$ (containing 1 and closed under multiplication), the localization $S^{-1}R$ consists of “fractions” $r/s$ with $r \in R$ , $s \in S$ , where $r_1/s_1 = r_2/s_2$ iff $t(r_1 s_2 - r_2 s_1) = 0$ for some $t \in S$ .

The most important case: $R = \mathbb{Z}$ , $S = \mathbb{Z} \setminus \{0\}$ . Then $S^{-1}\mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Q}$ — the rationals are the localization of the integers at the set of all nonzero elements. This is the field of fractions of $\mathbb{Z}$ .

More generally, every integral domain $R$ embeds into its field of fractions $\text{Frac}(R)$ . For $R = F[x]$ , the field of fractions is $F(x)$ — the field of rational functions in $x$ . Localization appears throughout algebraic geometry and number theory, though we will not pursue it further in this series.

What’s next#

We’ve seen that $R/M$ is a field when $M$ is maximal, and that $F[x]/(p(x))$ constructs a field in which $p(x)$ has a root. This is the gateway to field extensions — the systematic study of building larger fields from smaller ones by adjoining roots. Next: fields, extension degrees, algebraic and transcendental elements, and the question of which geometric constructions are possible with ruler and compass.
