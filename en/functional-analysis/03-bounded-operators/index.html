Bounded Linear Operators and Functionals

Bounded operators are the morphisms of functional analysis — continuity and linearity conspire to produce surprising rigidity.

Operators as the objects of study#

In finite dimensions, once you pick a basis, every linear map is a matrix. You can write it down, compute its eigenvalues, and go home. In infinite dimensions, “writing down” an operator is usually impossible, and the spectral theory is vastly richer. The first step: figure out which linear maps are well-behaved.

Bounded linear operators#

Let $X, Y$ be normed spaces. A linear map $T: X \to Y$ is bounded if:

$$\|T\| := \sup_{\|x\| \le 1} \|Tx\| < \infty.$$

Equivalently: there exists $C > 0$ such that $\|Tx\| \le C\|x\|$ for all $x$ .

Theorem. For a linear map between normed spaces, bounded $\iff$ continuous $\iff$ continuous at $0$ .

Proof. Bounded $\Rightarrow$ continuous: $\|Tx - Ty\| = \|T(x-y)\| \le \|T\|\,\|x-y\|$ , so $T$ is Lipschitz. Continuous at $0$ $\Rightarrow$ bounded: if $T$ is unbounded, find $x_n$ with $\|x_n\| = 1$ and $\|Tx_n\| > n$ . Then $x_n/n \to 0$ but $\|T(x_n/n)\| > 1$ , contradicting continuity at $0$ . $\square$

In finite dimensions, every linear map is bounded. In infinite dimensions, unbounded linear operators exist and are important (differential operators in PDE, the momentum operator in quantum mechanics), but they require delicate domain considerations. For now, we focus on bounded ones.

The operator norm and $B(X, Y)$ #

The space $B(X, Y)$ of all bounded linear operators from $X$ to $Y$ is itself a normed space under the operator norm. Moreover:

Theorem. If $Y$ is Banach, then $B(X, Y)$ is Banach (regardless of whether $X$ is complete).

Proof sketch. Let $(T_n)$ be Cauchy in $B(X, Y)$ . For each $x$ , $(T_n x)$ is Cauchy in $Y$ (since $\|T_n x - T_m x\| \le \|T_n - T_m\|\,\|x\|$ ). Define $Tx = \lim T_n x$ . Linearity is clear. Boundedness: $\|T_n\|$ is Cauchy in $\mathbb{R}$ , hence bounded by some $M$ . So $\|Tx\| = \lim \|T_n x\| \le M\|x\|$ . Convergence in operator norm: standard $\varepsilon/3$ argument. $\square$

Computing the operator norm — worked examples.

Example 1: The right shift. On $\ell^2$ , define $S(x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots) = (0, x_1, x_2, \ldots)$ . Then $\|Sx\|_2 = \|x\|_2$ for all $x$ , so $\|S\| = 1$ . $S$ is an isometry but not surjective. No finite-dimensional isometry fails to be surjective — this is a purely infinite-dimensional phenomenon.

Example 2: The left shift. $L(x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots) = (x_2, x_3, \ldots)$ . For the norm: $\|Lx\|_2^2 = \sum_{n=2}^\infty |x_n|^2 \le \|x\|_2^2$ , so $\|L\| \le 1$ . Equality is achieved by $x = (0, 1, 0, \ldots)$ : $\|Lx\| = 1 = \|x\|$ . Hence $\|L\| = 1$ . Note $LS = I$ but $SL \ne I$ (information is lost). The shifts are adjoints of each other.

Example 3: Integration operator. On $C[0,1]$ with sup norm, define $(Tf)(s) = \int_0^s f(t)\,dt$ . Then $\|Tf\|_\infty \le \sup_{s} \int_0^s |f(t)|\,dt \le \|f\|_\infty$ . So $\|T\| \le 1$ . For the lower bound, take $f \equiv 1$ : $Tf(s) = s$ , so $\|Tf\|_\infty = 1 = \|f\|_\infty$ . Hence $\|T\| = 1$ .

Example 4: Multiplication operators. On $L^2[0,1]$ , define $(M_\varphi f)(t) = \varphi(t) f(t)$ for $\varphi \in L^\infty[0,1]$ . Then $\|M_\varphi f\|_2 \le \|\varphi\|_\infty \|f\|_2$ , so $\|M_\varphi\| \le \|\varphi\|_\infty$ . Conversely, for any $\varepsilon > 0$ , the set $E = \{t : |\varphi(t)| > \|\varphi\|_\infty - \varepsilon\}$ has positive measure, and $f = \chi_E / \|\chi_E\|_2$ gives $\|M_\varphi f\|_2 > (\|\varphi\|_\infty - \varepsilon)$ . So $\|M_\varphi\| = \|\varphi\|_\infty$ . These are the “diagonal” operators — the spectral theorem says every normal operator looks like one of these on the right measure space.

The Neumann series#

The operator analogue of the geometric series. If $\|T\| < 1$ in $B(X)$ where $X$ is Banach, then $I - T$ is invertible with:

$$(I - T)^{-1} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty T^n.$$

Proof. The series converges absolutely: $\sum \|T^n\| \le \sum \|T\|^n = 1/(1 - \|T\|) < \infty$ . Since $B(X)$ is Banach, the series converges in operator norm to some $S$ . Check: $(I-T)S = \lim_{N} (I-T)\sum_{n=0}^N T^n = \lim_N (I - T^{N+1}) = I$ . Similarly $S(I-T) = I$ . $\square$

Application: perturbation of invertible operators. If $A$ is invertible and $\|B\| < 1/\|A^{-1}\|$ , then $A + B$ is invertible. Write $A + B = A(I + A^{-1}B)$ , and $\|A^{-1}B\| \le \|A^{-1}\|\|B\| < 1$ . The Neumann series gives $(I + A^{-1}B)^{-1}$ . This means the set of invertible operators in $B(X)$ is open — invertibility is stable under small perturbations. This fact underpins the stability of numerical methods for solving linear operator equations.

The dual space#

The most important special case: $Y = \mathbb{C}$ (or $\mathbb{R}$ ). A bounded linear functional is a bounded linear map $f: X \to \mathbb{C}$ . The dual space is:

$$X^* = B(X, \mathbb{C}).$$

By the theorem above, $X^*$ is always Banach (since $\mathbb{C}$ is complete), even if $X$ isn’t.

Identifying duals — the $\ell^p$ story. For $1 \le p < \infty$ , the dual of $\ell^p$ is $\ell^q$ where $1/p + 1/q = 1$ :

$$(\ell^p)^* \cong \ell^q, \quad f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty x_n y_n \text{ for unique } y \in \ell^q.$$

The isomorphism is isometric: $\|f\| = \|y\|_q$ . For $p = 2$ , we get $(\ell^2)^* \cong \ell^2$ — that’s the Riesz representation theorem. For $p = 1$ , the dual is $\ell^\infty$ .

But $(\ell^\infty)^* \ne \ell^1$ . The dual of $\ell^\infty$ is strictly larger — it contains “Banach limits” and other exotic functionals that can’t be written as convergent series. This asymmetry is a key feature of infinite-dimensional analysis.

Weak convergence#

The dual space gives us a weaker notion of convergence. A sequence $(x_n)$ in $X$ converges weakly to $x$ (written $x_n \rightharpoonup x$ ) if:

$$f(x_n) \to f(x) \quad \forall f \in X^*.$$

Strong (norm) convergence implies weak convergence, but not conversely.

Example 5. In $\ell^2$ , the standard basis vectors $e_n \rightharpoonup 0$ weakly (since $\langle e_n, y \rangle = y_n \to 0$ for any $y \in \ell^2$ ), but $\|e_n\| = 1 \not\to 0$ . The unit sphere in infinite dimensions is weakly “floppy” — sequences on it can converge weakly to the origin.

This never happens in finite dimensions. In $\mathbb{R}^n$ , weak and strong convergence coincide. The distinction is purely infinite-dimensional, and it matters enormously for variational problems (minimizing sequences converge weakly but not strongly).

Compact operators#

An operator $T: X \to Y$ is compact if it maps bounded sets to relatively compact sets (sets with compact closure). Equivalently, every bounded sequence $(x_n)$ has a subsequence such that $(Tx_{n_k})$ converges.

In finite dimensions, every bounded operator is compact. In infinite dimensions, compact operators are “almost finite-dimensional” — they can be approximated by finite-rank operators.

Example 6. On $\ell^2$ , the diagonal operator $T(x_1, x_2, \ldots) = (x_1, x_2/2, x_3/3, \ldots)$ is compact. To see this: the finite-rank operators $T_N(x_1, \ldots) = (x_1, x_2/2, \ldots, x_N/N, 0, 0, \ldots)$ satisfy $\|T - T_N\| = 1/(N+1) \to 0$ . Since compact operators form a closed set, $T$ is compact.

Non-example: the identity. $I: \ell^2 \to \ell^2$ is not compact: the sequence $e_n$ is bounded but has no convergent subsequence since $\|e_n - e_m\| = \sqrt{2}$ for $n \ne m$ .

Compact operators form a closed two-sided ideal in $B(H)$ : if $T$ is compact and $A$ is bounded, then $AT$ and $TA$ are compact. This ideal structure means compactness is preserved under “sandwiching” by bounded operators. The quotient algebra $B(H)/K(H)$ (the Calkin algebra) is where index theory lives — but that’s a story for another day.

Hilbert-Schmidt operators. On $L^2[0,1]$ , integral operators $(Tf)(s) = \int_0^1 k(s,t)f(t)\,dt$ with kernel $k \in L^2([0,1]^2)$ are compact. These are called Hilbert-Schmidt operators, and they satisfy $\|T\|^2 \le \int\int |k(s,t)|^2\,ds\,dt$ . Every Hilbert-Schmidt operator is compact, but not conversely — compactness is the weaker condition. The Hilbert-Schmidt norm is easier to compute from the kernel, making it a practical tool for verifying compactness.

Adjoint operators#

For $T \in B(X, Y)$ , the adjoint $T^*: Y^* \to X^*$ is defined by:

$$(T^* g)(x) = g(Tx), \quad g \in Y^*, \ x \in X.$$

This is the “transpose” generalized. Always $\|T^*\| = \|T\|$ .

For Hilbert spaces, the Riesz theorem lets us identify the adjoint as an operator $T^*: H \to H$ satisfying $\langle Tx, y \rangle = \langle x, T^* y \rangle$ . The right shift and left shift on $\ell^2$ are adjoints: $S^* = L$ .

Self-adjoint operators ($T = T^*$ ) have real spectrum — the infinite-dimensional analogue of symmetric matrices having real eigenvalues. They’re the operators of quantum mechanics (observables). Normal operators ($TT^* = T^*T$ ) include self-adjoint and unitary operators, and admit the fullest spectral theory.

Example 7: Computing adjoints. For the Volterra integral operator $(Vf)(s) = \int_0^s f(t)\,dt$ on $L^2[0,1]$ :

$$\langle Vf, g \rangle = \int_0^1 \left(\int_0^s f(t)\,dt\right) \overline{g(s)}\,ds = \int_0^1 f(t)\left(\int_t^1 \overline{g(s)}\,ds\right)dt = \langle f, V^*g \rangle,$$

so $(V^*g)(t) = \int_t^1 g(s)\,ds$ . The adjoint “integrates from the right” instead of from the left. Note $V \ne V^*$ , so $V$ is not self-adjoint. In fact $V$ has no eigenvalues at all (one can check directly), yet $\sigma(V) = \{0\}$ — a purely continuous spectrum concentrated at a single point.

Connections: operators in physics and engineering#

Quantum mechanics. The position operator $(\hat{x}\psi)(x) = x\psi(x)$ on $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ is unbounded (it’s a multiplication operator by an unbounded function). The momentum operator $\hat{p} = -i\hbar\, d/dx$ is also unbounded. These are defined on dense domains (Schwartz space, typically) and are self-adjoint on those domains. The bounded operator theory tells us what well-behaved operators look like; the unbounded theory (built atop it) handles the physically essential ones.

Signal processing. A linear time-invariant (LTI) system is precisely a bounded operator on $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ that commutes with translations. The spectral theorem identifies these with multiplication operators in the Fourier domain: $(Tf)^\wedge(\xi) = H(\xi)\hat{f}(\xi)$ for some $H \in L^\infty$ (the transfer function). The operator norm $\|T\| = \|H\|_\infty$ is the maximum gain of the filter. Stability of the system is equivalent to boundedness of the operator.

Numerical linear algebra. The condition number of a matrix $A$ is $\kappa(A) = \|A\|\,\|A^{-1}\|$ . The Neumann series tells us that perturbations $A + \delta A$ remain invertible when $\|\delta A\| < 1/\|A^{-1}\|$ , i.e., when the relative perturbation $\|\delta A\|/\|A\| < 1/\kappa(A)$ . Ill-conditioned systems (large $\kappa$ ) are sensitive to perturbations — this is the operator-theoretic explanation of why Gaussian elimination can fail in floating point.

What’s next#

We’ve described the players: spaces and operators. Now comes the power — the four fundamental theorems that make the whole theory work. Hahn-Banach says dual spaces are rich. Open mapping says surjective operators are well-behaved. Closed graph says you can check boundedness indirectly. Uniform boundedness says pointwise bounds give uniform bounds. Each one relies on completeness in an essential way.
