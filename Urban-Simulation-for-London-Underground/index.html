Introduction

This article is to use different urban simulation methods to model the resilience of London’s underground and the spatial interaction. The first part is using several impact measures, global efficiency and connected component for example, to model the removal of nodes according to the rank of several centrality measures. The second part try to decide the spatial interaction models that is suitable for several scenarios.

¶Centrality measures

The three centrality we choose to use to distinguish nodes is degree centrality, betweenness centrality and closeness centrality. Centrality is a concept used in social network analysis to measure the degree to which a point in the network or a person is close to the center in the entire network. That is to say, by knowing the centrality of a node, the importance of this node in the network can be judged.

In this standardized degree centrality measurement formula, we use the degree centrality value of node divided by the maximum possible number of connections of other nodes to obtain the proportion of network nodes that are directly connected to node . The larger the ratio, the higher the centrality. For our London underground network, if a station has a higher degree centrality, it will have more links to other stations.

Betweenness centrality is used to measure the number of times a vertex appears in the shortest path between any other pair of vertices, thereby characterizing node importance. When it comes to London underground, if a station is between multiple stations, it can be considered that the node plays an important “intermediary” role, and the station in this position can control the nearby stations and influence the group.

¶Impact measures

The two methods to measure impact of removal we choose here is global efficiency and connected component.

Global efficiency is the average of the inverse shortest path lengths of all node pairs. Such metrics can be viewed as measures of the efficiency with which information is exchanged in the network. Assuming that is the shortest path of node and node , then the definition is as follows:

When every station in the London underground network is reachable to each other, the global efficiency is the largest. And when the shortest path becomes longer, the global efficiency will gradually decrease. Thus removing nodes my cause the global efficiency to decrease.

Number of connected subgraphs has the same meaning of largest connected component. Since it’s much easier for networkx package to implement it, this measurement is adopted instead of computing the common-see largest connected component. Removing some key nodes in London underground network may cause the whole graph to be split into two or more parts, which will increase the number of connected subgraphs.

¶Node removal

For the centrality measures we choose, two removal strategies are adopted to remove the nodes one by one. Strategy A will remove the nodes according to the nodes ranked, namely non-sequential removal. While strategy B remove one node a time and recompute the most important one after the removal, and remove it accordingly next time. Strategy B is so-called sequential removal.

The comparison graph of Strategy A is shown in figure below:

It can be seen that removing nodes according to the three centrality measures will lead to a rapid decline in global efficiency, but removing nodes according to closeness centrality does not increase the connected component, that is, the graph obtained after removing the nodes is still a connected graph, and the stations are still connected to each other. reachable. In contrast, removing nodes according to the other two centralities will immediately make the graph disconnected, indicating that the importance of removing nodes by these two methods has a greater impact on the connectivity of the whole graph than closeness centrality. It can also be seen in the graph of global efficiency that the removal of nodes selected according to closeness centrality has a smaller impact on the whole graph than the other two methods. Thus the degree centrality and betweenness centrality are both good choice for reflecting the importance of a station for the functioning of the underground.

The comparison of Strategy B is shown in figure below:

Compared to the previous figure, we can find that strategies B has significantly higher declines in global efficiency and higher connected components than strategy A. Actually this is quite understandable. Because strategy B actually finds the most important node under the current centrality measure for deletion every time a node is deleted, strategy A relies on the order of the nodes selected at the beginning to delete, thus ignoring different nodes in the deletion process change in importance. Therefore, I think Strategy B can more truly reflect the importance ranking of deleting nodes, thus is more effective at studying resilience.

As far as I am concern, directly analyzing the number of connected subgraphs is a better way to assess the damage. Because its meaning is more intuitive and obvious, it represents whether removing a node will affect the connectivity of the entire graph and the magnitude of the effect (how many subgraphs are formed). In contrast, in the global efficiency, although we can see that the value has been decreasing, it is difficult for us to directly see which node deletion has a greater impact.

¶Flows: weighted network

The degree centrality and measurement discussed above ignored the influence of the flows between stations. We only take the connection activity into account.

¶Centrality measures

Since we need to use the distance between nodes as a metric in closeness centrality, the flow between stations is provided in the original data. We convert the 0 part of the flows to a relatively small value (such as 1), and then take the inverse of the flows to get the distance.
